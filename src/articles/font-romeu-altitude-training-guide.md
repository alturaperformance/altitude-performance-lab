# Font Romeu Altitude Training: Europe's Premier High-Altitude Camp Destination

Font Romeu altitude training has shaped European endurance athletics for over half a century. Perched at 1,850 meters (6,070 feet) in the French Pyrenees near the Spanish border, Font Romeu was purpose-built as a high-performance sports complex in the lead-up to the 1968 Mexico City Olympics — themselves held at 2,240m — and has been a cornerstone of European altitude methodology ever since. From French Olympic sprinters to British marathon hopefuls, from Spanish cyclists to East African distance runners based in Europe, Font Romeu draws athletes precisely because it marries proven hypoxic stimulus with world-class infrastructure.

## The Physiology Behind Font Romeu's Effectiveness

At 1,850m, Font Romeu sits at the lower boundary of what researchers consider the optimal altitude band for hematological adaptation (roughly 1,800–2,500m). The partial pressure of oxygen here is approximately 118 mmHg, compared to 159 mmHg at sea level — a reduction of about 26%.

This modest but sustained hypoxic stimulus activates the key adaptive pathways:

- **HIF-1α upregulation:** Hypoxia-inducible factor 1-alpha drives transcription of erythropoietin (EPO) genes, primarily in renal interstitial cells.
- **EPO surge:** Serum EPO begins rising within 6–12 hours of arrival, peaks at 24–48 hours, and stabilizes at an elevated plateau through the first 2–3 weeks before leveling off if the athlete remains at altitude.
- **Hemoglobin mass expansion:** Research by Wehrlin and colleagues (2006, *European Journal of Applied Physiology*) demonstrated hemoglobin mass increases of approximately 1% per week for the first 3 weeks in athletes training at altitudes comparable to Font Romeu's.
- **Mitochondrial and enzymatic adaptations:** Training at this elevation maintains exercise quality closely enough to sea-level work that mitochondrial biogenesis, capillary density, and oxidative enzyme activity continue to develop alongside hematological gains.

Critically, 1,850m is also low enough that VO2 max declines are modest (approximately 5–8%), allowing athletes to sustain the high-quality training sessions that stimulate neuromuscular and metabolic adaptations alongside the altitude stimulus.

## Facilities: The INSEP-Affiliated National Center

Font Romeu's centerpiece is the **Centre National d'Entraînement en Altitude (CNEA)**, operated under the umbrella of the French Institute for Sport (INSEP). The facility includes:

- **Olympic-standard synthetic athletics track (400m):** Used by French and international track and field athletes year-round.
- **400m indoor hall:** Allows continuation of track work in inclement Pyrenean weather.
- **Heated competition pool:** Open-water and swimming disciplines benefit from altitude-level hemoglobin adaptations too.
- **Strength and conditioning suites:** Full resistance training provision, including Olympic lifting platforms and comprehensive gym equipment.
- **Sports medicine and physiology labs:** On-site blood testing, VO2 max testing, and body composition assessment.
- **Accommodations and catering:** Dormitory-style lodging with performance nutrition catering.
- **Cross-country and trail routes:** Extensive networks on the surrounding Cerdagne plateau and mountain slopes.

The CNEA primarily serves French national teams and federation-affiliated programs, but international bookings are accepted, particularly during off-peak periods. Private training groups and individual elite athletes can access facilities through the INSEP coordination framework.

Beyond the national center, Font Romeu's wider village has hotels, gîtes (French country lodges), and rental accommodations used by independent training groups.

## Who Trains in Font Romeu

Font Romeu's client list reads like a European Olympic delegation:

- **French athletics:** The French Athletics Federation has used Font Romeu as its primary altitude preparation venue since the 1970s. Multiple French Olympic medalists in middle distance and road racing have trained here.
- **British Athletics:** UK endurance athletes, particularly middle-distance runners, have staged pre-championship altitude blocks in Font Romeu.
- **Spanish cycling:** The Cerdagne plateau's road network draws professional cycling teams for altitude training camps and pre-Grand Tour preparation.
- **Swedish and Nordic nations:** Nordic combined, biathlon, and cross-country skiing athletes use Font Romeu for summer dry-land altitude training and VO2 max development blocks.
- **African athletes based in Europe:** Kenyan and Ethiopian distance runners who compete on the European circuit frequently use Font Romeu for mid-season altitude refreshers.

The camp's prestige also draws academic sport science groups — it has been the site of numerous published altitude physiology studies, which has reinforced its evidence-based reputation.

## Planning Your Font Romeu Training Block

### Duration and Timing

The minimum effective altitude block is **21 days**, with 28 days considered optimal for maximal hemoglobin mass adaptation. Most European national program camps run 3–5 weeks.

For race timing, the traditional guidance remains:
- Race within **2–21 days** of descent, targeting the performance window after acute altitude fatigue resolves.
- A second window exists at **14–21 days** post-descent when residual hematological benefits peak before washout commences.

### Periodization Within the Camp

**Week 1 (Adaptation):** Volume reduction of 20–25%. No high-intensity sessions. Focus on easy aerobic running, building familiarity with the terrain and establishing baseline SpO2 and HRV readings.

**Week 2 (Progressive loading):** Introduce tempo work and moderate-intensity intervals. Watch HR carefully — at altitude, the same pace elicits a higher cardiac output. Use power meters (cyclists) or heart rate (runners) rather than pace to guide intensity.

**Week 3–4 (Full training):** Return to near-full training volume and intensity. This is where the physiological work is done — the body is acclimatized sufficiently to tolerate quality sessions while the EPO and hemoglobin mass response is at its peak.

**Final 3–5 days:** Taper volume for the journey home and to arrive fresh for the recovery phase.

### Environmental Conditions at Font Romeu

- **Temperature:** Summer highs of 20–25°C (68–77°F) with cool nights (5–12°C / 41–54°F). Winter sees sub-zero temperatures; Font Romeu also operates as a ski resort.
- **Precipitation:** Pyrenean weather is variable. Afternoon thunderstorms are common in July–August.
- **Sunshine:** The Cerdagne valley is one of the sunniest regions in France — Font Romeu averages 3,000 hours of sunshine per year, which facilitates outdoor track and road training.
- **Terrain:** The plateau surrounding Font Romeu is gently rolling at altitude, with steeper mountain ascents available for strength work. Road cyclists benefit from multiple cols (mountain passes) accessible directly from the village.

### Logistics

- **Access:** Font Romeu is a 3-hour drive from Barcelona, 4 hours from Toulouse. The nearest international airport is Barcelona El Prat (BCN) or Perpignan (PGF) for smaller aircraft. A train-bus combination via Perpignan is viable.
- **Best seasons for training camps:** April–October for athletics; November–April for skiing and winter sports.
- **Language:** French predominates, though English is spoken in sports facility contexts. Spanish is commonly understood given proximity to Catalonia.

## Monitoring Progress at Font Romeu

Effective altitude camps are not passive — they require ongoing physiological monitoring to confirm the adaptive response is occurring and training load is appropriate.

**Key metrics to track:**

| Metric | Tool | Target Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Morning SpO2 | Pulse oximeter | Rising from Day 1 baseline; >93% after Day 7 |
| Resting HR | Wearable or manual | Elevated first week; returning toward baseline by Day 10 |
| HRV | WHOOP, Garmin, Oura Ring | Depression >15% from baseline = reduce load |
| Serum ferritin | Sports medicine clinic | >30 ng/mL; confirm before camp begins |
| Training paces/power | GPS watch / power meter | Expect 5–10% slower at comparable HR until acclimatized |

On-site labs at CNEA can provide serum EPO and hemoglobin mass measurements (via carbon monoxide rebreathing technique), which is a significant advantage over most altitude camps worldwide.

## Font Romeu vs. Other European Altitude Venues

| Location | Country | Elevation | Primary Sports |
|---|---|---|---|
| Font Romeu | France | 1,850m | Athletics, cycling, Nordic |
| St. Moritz | Switzerland | 1,822m | Athletics, cycling |
| Livigno | Italy | 1,816m | Cycling, Nordic, biathlon |
| Sierra Nevada | Spain | 2,320m | Athletics, cycling |
| Iten | Kenya | 2,400m | Distance running |

Font Romeu's edge over St. Moritz and Livigno is primarily infrastructural — the CNEA is purpose-built for high-performance sport in a way that hotels-converted-to-camps are not. Sierra Nevada offers a slightly higher elevation (and thus slightly stronger hypoxic stimulus) but is more remote. For European athletes seeking a proven, logistically efficient venue, Font Romeu remains the default choice.

## Common Mistakes Athletes Make at Font Romeu

**Going too hard too soon.** The 1,850m elevation feels manageable — it's not dramatic like Iten or Nairobi — and athletes underestimate its physiological cost. Maintaining sea-level training intensity in Week 1 is a reliable way to accumulate fatigue and suppress the immune system.

**Underhydrating.** Altitude increases insensible water loss via respiration. Aim for 3+ liters daily in the first week.

**Ignoring iron status.** A 2014 audit of British Athletics altitude camps found that a significant minority of athletes arrived at altitude with ferritin levels too low to mount a robust EPO response. A sports blood panel 4–6 weeks before departure is essential preparation.

**Racing too soon after descent.** The acute altitude fatigue window (roughly Days 1–5 post-descent) is real. Athletes who race within 48 hours of returning often underperform despite elevated hemoglobin.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Font Romeu's 1,850m provides a genuine hypoxic stimulus** — sufficient to drive EPO, hemoglobin mass, and mitochondrial adaptations over a 3–4 week block.
2. **The CNEA's facilities are world-class** — athletics track, indoor hall, pool, strength lab, and sports medicine support are all on-site.
3. **Plan a 3–4 week minimum stay**, paced conservatively in Week 1 with progressive loading thereafter.
4. **Iron, hydration, and sleep management are non-negotiable.** Confirm ferritin before you go.
5. **Target racing 2–21 days post-descent** to exploit the hematological window.
