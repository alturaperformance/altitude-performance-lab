# Jet Lag + Altitude: How to Manage Travel Fatigue When Flying to a High-Altitude Camp

Flying to an altitude training camp is a logistical achievement and a physiological assault. By the time most athletes arrive at their destination — a 2,400 m camp in the Andes, a 2,100 m base in Flagstaff, a 1,800 m facility in Font Romeu — they have already spent 8–20 hours in pressurized aircraft cabin air, crossed multiple time zones, eaten at irregular times, slept poorly, and arrived mildly dehydrated. Then they step off the plane and immediately begin acclimatizing to altitude.

The combination of **jet lag and altitude** is not merely additive — it is synergistic in ways that can derail the first week of a camp before training even begins. Managing this dual stressor systematically is one of the highest-leverage preparation strategies available to altitude athletes, and it is almost universally underestimated.

## The Physiology of Jet Lag

Jet lag results from misalignment between the body's internal circadian clock and the external light-dark cycle at the destination. The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) — the master clock — requires time to resynchronize to a new time zone at a rate of approximately 1 hour per day for eastward travel and 1.5 hours per day for westward travel.

During this resynchronization period, athletes experience:

- **Disrupted sleep architecture:** Difficulty falling asleep, early awakening, or hypersomnia at inappropriate times
- **Suppressed alertness:** Daytime cognitive impairment, reduced processing speed, and impaired working memory
- **Dysregulated hormonal rhythms:** Cortisol, testosterone, melatonin, and growth hormone — all critical for training and recovery — are secreted on circadian schedules that take 7–14 days to fully re-entrain
- **GI disruption:** Appetite dysregulation, bowel irregularity, and altered gut motility
- **Reduced exercise performance:** Several meta-analyses show that jet lag from eastward travel reduces both aerobic and anaerobic performance by 3–7% in the first 2–5 days at destination

## Why Altitude Compounds Jet Lag

Altitude imposes its own physiological stress, detailed throughout this site. The mechanisms that make jet lag and altitude particularly problematic in combination:

### Sleep Disruption Is Doubled

Jet lag disrupts the timing of sleep. Altitude disrupts the quality of sleep through periodic breathing (Cheyne-Stokes respiration) and frequent nocturnal arousal. An athlete arriving at a high-altitude destination with significant jet lag faces sleep disruption from two independent mechanisms simultaneously. Sleep is the primary driver of adaptation and cognitive recovery — when it is severely disrupted in the first 5–10 days, training adaptation suffers proportionally.

### Cortisol Dysregulation and Training Stress

The cortisol spike of jet lag — elevated cortisol at inappropriate times due to circadian misalignment — intersects with the elevated cortisol of altitude acclimatization. Both conditions are individually catabolic. Combined, they create a cortisol environment that suppresses anabolic processes, increases protein catabolism, and impairs immune function. Athletes who begin hard training into this state are at elevated risk for [non-functional overreaching](overtraining-at-altitude-prevention.md).

### Cognitive Impairment Is Amplified

Jet lag impairs working memory, reaction time, and executive function through sleep disruption and circadian misalignment. Altitude independently impairs the same cognitive domains through hypoxia. The combined effect on [cognitive performance at altitude](cognitive-effects-altitude-athletes.md) is greater than either stressor alone.

### Dehydration From Aircraft Cabin Air

Pressurized aircraft cabins typically maintain humidity at 5–15%, compared to 40–60% typical of comfortable indoor environments. A transatlantic or transpacific flight of 8–12 hours produces significant respiratory and skin surface water loss — estimates range from 500 ml to over 1,000 ml depending on cabin humidity, individual variation, and flight duration. Arriving at altitude in a state of pre-existing dehydration accelerates altitude diuresis effects and makes [hydration management](hydration-strategy-altitude-athletes.md) even more critical in the first 24 hours.

## Time Zone Direction Matters

The direction of travel significantly affects jet lag severity at altitude:

- **Eastward travel** (e.g., North America to Europe for Font Romeu; Europe to East Africa for Kenyan camps) is generally worse, requiring phase advancement of the circadian clock, which the body resists.
- **Westward travel** (e.g., Europe to Flagstaff; Europe to South American altitude camps) is typically better tolerated, requiring phase delay, which is more natural.
- **North-south travel with minimal time zone crossing** (e.g., North America to Colorado, or Europe to Font Romeu with minimal zone shift) produces minimal jet lag despite long travel times.

Athletes traveling eastward across 6+ time zones to an altitude camp face the worst combined stressor scenario. A specific protocol is warranted.

## A Framework for Managing Jet Lag + Altitude

### Pre-Travel: Begin Shifting Before You Leave

For eastward travel of 4+ time zones:

- **3–5 days before departure:** Begin shifting your sleep schedule earlier by 30–60 minutes per day. If you're traveling to a camp that is 6 time zones east, shifting 2–3 hours of your schedule before departure means only 3–4 hours of resynchronization remain at destination.
- **Light exposure:** Morning bright light exposure advances your clock. Use this deliberately in the days before departure.
- **Melatonin:** Low-dose melatonin (0.5–3 mg) taken at the target destination bedtime in the days before departure assists phase shifting. Note: melatonin is a timing agent, not a sleeping pill. Use it consistently at the same adjusted time.

For westward travel, pre-shifting is less important. Focus on staying awake until local bedtime on arrival day.

### During Travel

- **Hydrate aggressively:** Target 300–400 ml per hour of flight, with electrolytes. Avoid alcohol (depresses sleep quality and increases dehydration) and minimize caffeine in the final 6 hours before your destination's bedtime.
- **Sleep on the plane according to destination time:** If it is nighttime at your destination, sleep during the flight. Use earplugs, eye masks, and a neck pillow. If it is daytime at your destination, limit sleep on the flight to short naps and stay as alert as possible.
- **Move regularly:** Circulation during long flights reduces deep vein thrombosis risk and helps maintain alertness when staying awake is the goal.
- **Eat according to destination time zones:** Meal timing provides a powerful zeitgeber (time cue) for circadian entrainment. If your destination is 6 hours ahead, eating meals at the destination-adjusted time during the flight accelerates clock resetting.

### Arrival: Days 1–3

This is the highest-risk window. You are simultaneously jet-lagged and acclimatizing. Specific recommendations:

**Avoid intense training for the first 48 hours.** The combination of disrupted cortisol rhythms, impaired sleep, dehydration, and early altitude acclimatization creates conditions where even moderate training can tip the balance toward overreaching. Easy walking, light stretching, and sightseeing at altitude provide the hypoxic stimulus without adding significant training stress.

**Establish immediate light exposure at destination.** On the first morning at the camp, get outside in bright sunlight before 9 a.m. local time. Light is the primary zeitgeber and the fastest way to anchor your circadian clock to the new time zone.

**Do not nap more than 20 minutes** in the first 2 days (unless crossing 8+ time zones eastward). Short naps preserve sleep pressure for the nighttime anchor. Long daytime naps at altitude will make nighttime periodic breathing worse by reducing sleep pressure, fragmenting the sleep cycle further.

**Target bedtime at local time regardless of internal clock signals.** Force the social entrainment cue even if the internal clock protests. Darkness and melatonin at local bedtime accelerate resynchronization.

**Front-load carbohydrate and protein intake.** Appetite is typically poor in the combined jet lag + altitude window. Small, carbohydrate-rich meals every 3–4 hours are easier to tolerate than large meals and help maintain blood glucose and training availability.

### Days 4–7: Gradual Introduction of Training Stress

By day 4, most athletes feel the acute jet lag beginning to resolve (for 4–6 hour zone crossings) or meaningfully improve (for 8+ hour crossings). Altitude acclimatization is also underway but not complete. This is the phase for:

- **Aerobic base work** at reduced volume and intensity
- Continuing to monitor resting heart rate and HRV daily
- Building sleep duration and quality as the primary recovery target
- Structured meals at local times to reinforce circadian entrainment

### Days 7–14: Normal Altitude Camp Protocol

By week 2, jet lag should be largely resolved for most athletes. The dominant challenge is now altitude acclimatization alone. Follow standard [altitude recovery protocols](altitude-training-recovery-guide.md).

## Specific Scenarios and Recommendations

### Scenario 1: Domestic Travel (Same Time Zone)

Athletes flying domestically to a high-altitude destination with no significant time zone crossing (e.g., Denver, Colorado from the US East Coast; driving to a European mountain camp with 1–2 hour time difference) face altitude stress without significant jet lag. The focus is:

- Hydrate well during travel
- Arrive at least 24 hours before any intensive training
- No special pre-travel circadian manipulation needed

### Scenario 2: Moderate Jet Lag (3–5 Time Zones)

Common scenario: European athletes flying to Flagstaff (6–8 time zone crossing eastward for return journey), North American athletes flying to European camps.

- Pre-shift sleep schedule 2–3 days before departure (45–60 min/day advance for eastward travel)
- Melatonin at destination bedtime from the night of arrival for 3–5 nights
- Restrict training to light aerobic work in days 1–3

### Scenario 3: Severe Jet Lag (6+ Time Zones) to High Altitude

Worst-case scenario: Athletes flying from North America or Europe to East African altitude camps (Iten, Kenya at 2,400 m; Addis Ababa area at 2,500 m), often involving 8–12 time zone crossings and 15–24 hours of total travel.

- Aggressive pre-travel shifting beginning 5–7 days before departure
- Strategic bright light and melatonin protocol during travel
- **Mandatory 3-day arrival buffer before any structured training**
- Consider arriving 4–5 days early to allow jet lag to resolve before altitude training proper begins
- Medical oversight for first-week symptoms given high risk of combined physiological stress

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Plan the travel, not just the training.** Jet lag at altitude is not background noise — it is a performance variable. Build arrival days into the camp plan.
2. **Direction of travel matters.** Eastward travel is harder to manage than westward. Adjust your pre-travel shifting protocol accordingly.
3. **Avoid intense training in the first 48 hours.** Combined jet lag and altitude stress is a setup for non-functional overreaching.
4. **Use light, meal timing, and melatonin** as the three primary tools for circadian resynchronization.
5. **Hydrate from the moment you board the aircraft.** Arrive at altitude in fluid balance, not already dehydrated.
6. **For 6+ hour eastward time zone crossings to high altitude**, a 4–5 day arrival buffer is not luxury — it is performance management.
