# Overtraining at Altitude: Warning Signs and How to Avoid Going Too Hard Too High

There is a particular type of altitude camp failure that is both common and preventable. It looks like this: an athlete arrives motivated, feels surprisingly decent in the first few days, starts hitting harder sessions by the end of week 1, and by day 10–12 is in a hole — legs that won't respond, resting heart rate elevated by 8–12 bpm, sleep falling apart, and sessions that produce exhaustion rather than adaptation. Three weeks later, they return from a camp having spent significant time and money on an experience that actually set them back.

This is **overtraining at altitude**, and it is more common than most athletes admit because altitude creates conditions where normal warning systems fail.

## Why Altitude Makes Overtraining Easier

At sea level, trained athletes have reasonable intuition about training load. You know roughly what a hard week feels like, how long it takes to recover, and what your body's warning signals mean. At altitude, all of those calibrations break down.

### Perceived Effort Becomes Unreliable

The same pace or power output at 2,400 m requires 10–20% more physiological effort than at sea level, depending on the athlete and the elevation. When athletes train by feel — or when they try to hit sea-level pace targets — they are systematically overloading their cardiovascular system without realizing it. Studies using matched RPE training at altitude consistently show higher heart rate, higher blood lactate, and greater sympathetic activation than the athlete perceives from subjective feel alone.

### Recovery Is Slower

Altitude suppresses anabolic hormones and elevates cortisol, particularly in the first 2 weeks. Testosterone-to-cortisol ratio — a useful proxy for anabolic/catabolic balance — is typically unfavorable at altitude in the first 1–2 weeks. This means the recovery from a training session takes longer than the athlete expects, and the accumulation of training stress between sessions grows faster than it would at sea level.

### Sleep Quality Degrades

Sleep is the primary recovery mechanism. At altitude, periodic breathing (Cheyne-Stokes respiration) and frequent nocturnal arousal reduce sleep quality and duration, particularly in the first 5–10 nights. Athletes who are accumulating fatigue cannot recover between sessions as efficiently as they can at sea level — and they may not even realize their sleep is fragmented, since altitude-related awakenings are often brief and poorly remembered.

### Competition and Social Pressure

Athletes at training camps often train together and face social pressure to keep up with training partners, hit the same paces as their sea-level sessions, or not appear to be "struggling." This culture of stoicism at altitude camps is one of the strongest predictors of overreaching.

## The Spectrum: Functional Overreaching vs. Overtraining Syndrome

It is important to distinguish between levels of training stress:

**Functional Overreaching (FOR):** A short-term accumulation of training load that temporarily depresses performance but from which the athlete rebounds with a 1–2 week taper or rest period. FOR is a normal and intended part of structured training. A well-planned altitude block may intentionally include a moderate overreach in week 2, followed by a recovery phase.

**Non-Functional Overreaching (NFOR):** Sustained training stress that causes performance decrements lasting 3–12 weeks. Athletes require extended rest and medical oversight. Altitude significantly lowers the threshold for tipping from FOR into NFOR.

**Overtraining Syndrome (OTS):** A severe, systemic condition requiring months of recovery. Rare but real, and almost always the result of NFOR that was not recognized and acted upon in time.

Most altitude-related overtraining falls into the NFOR category. The good news is that NFOR is detectable early — if you know what to look for.

## Warning Signs of Overtraining at Altitude

### Physiological Signals

- **Elevated resting heart rate:** A sustained increase of 6–10+ bpm above your normal morning resting HR is a reliable early warning. Track resting HR daily during camp — it is the simplest and most actionable indicator.
- **Declining HRV:** Heart rate variability drops reflect autonomic stress. If HRV falls consistently over 3–5 days without rebounding, training load should be reduced.
- **SpO₂ that fails to improve:** Resting SpO₂ should trend upward over the first 10–14 days of altitude exposure. If it plateaus or declines, your acclimatization is being disrupted by excess training stress.
- **Immunosuppression:** Frequent upper respiratory tract infections (URI) or unexplained illness are classic signs of overreaching. Altitude already suppresses immune function; excessive training compounds this significantly.

### Performance Signals

- **Pace/power at fixed RPE declining over consecutive sessions:** If RPE 6 effort produces lower pace or power on each successive day, recovery is not keeping pace with training stress.
- **Inability to complete prescribed intervals:** Dropping sets, significant deterioration in quality within a session, or inability to hit target zones despite adequate warm-up.
- **Extended time to feel "normal" in a session:** Athletes in a hole often describe the first 30–40 minutes of a session as feeling awful, then gradually improving. This is a sign of insufficient recovery.

### Psychological and Behavioral Signals

- **Mood deterioration:** Irritability, anxiety, or apathy toward training are well-documented in overreaching literature. The relationship is bidirectional — overreaching causes mood depression, and mood depression makes the training feel worse.
- **Loss of motivation:** Genuine lack of desire to train — not just a bad day, but a persistent reluctance — is a red flag.
- **Sleep disturbance:** Difficulty falling asleep (beyond altitude's typical periodic breathing effects) or early-morning waking that does not resolve after 7–10 days at altitude.
- **Appetite loss:** Caloric intake typically drops at altitude due to reduced appetite. Severe appetite suppression combined with high training volume is a setup for energy deficiency.

## How to Avoid Overtraining at Altitude

### 1. Reduce Volume in Week 1, Not Just Intensity

The standard guidance is to reduce intensity in the first week at altitude. This is correct but incomplete. Total training volume should also be reduced. A commonly cited guideline is a 10–20% reduction in both volume and intensity relative to your recent sea-level load, with gradual progression into week 2. Elite coaches working with national programs typically hold week-1 volume at 70–80% of sea-level targets.

### 2. Train by Effort, Not Pace or Power

Heart rate at altitude is elevated at any given pace. If you try to hit sea-level paces, you will be training in a higher intensity zone than intended. Use RPE and heart rate-based targets rather than power or pace targets in the first 1–2 weeks. Pace-based training can be reintroduced in week 2–3 when cardiovascular acclimatization improves functional capacity.

### 3. Add a Recovery Day After Every 2 Hard Days

At sea level, many competitive athletes train 6–7 days per week with only minimal active recovery. At altitude, the standard should be one full recovery day for every 2 days of genuine training stress. This is not optional — it is the mechanism through which the adaptation you sought at altitude actually consolidates.

### 4. Prioritize Sleep Aggressively

For more on sleep at altitude, see [why sleep suffers at altitude](sleep-quality-altitude-athletes.md). The short version: use every tool available — sleep environment optimization, avoiding alcohol and high-intensity training in the 3 hours before bed, and considering pharmacological support if periodic breathing is severe. A week of poor sleep at altitude will destroy a well-designed training plan.

### 5. Track Leading Indicators Daily

The simplest protocol: wake up, measure resting HR and HRV before rising. Note subjective wellness on a 1–5 scale (fatigue, mood, motivation, sleep quality). If two or more of these indicators are trending negatively over 3+ consecutive days, reduce training load by 30% and add a rest day before the next hard session.

### 6. Build In a Mid-Camp Recovery Period

For 3-week camps, a structured recovery micro-cycle around day 10–14 — where volume drops to 50–60% and intensity is low — allows a second-half peak rather than a mid-camp crash. Many elite programs design altitude blocks with this built in, labeling it "weeks 1–2 load, week 3 peak" or similar structures.

## A Note on the [Recovery Demands at Altitude](altitude-training-recovery-guide.md)

Overtraining at altitude is ultimately a failure of recovery management. The training stimulus at altitude is inherently higher than at sea level — the goal is not to push more training stress into that environment, but to absorb the heightened stimulus already present. Less is frequently more.

The athletes who return from altitude camps with legitimate adaptations — faster acclimatization on return, improved Hbmass, better buffering capacity — are not the ones who trained the hardest. They are the ones who trained optimally and recovered enough to let the adaptations consolidate.

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Measure resting HR every morning.** A 6+ bpm sustained increase is a warning sign requiring load reduction.
2. **Cut week-1 volume by 15–20%** relative to recent sea-level training, not just intensity.
3. **Train by RPE and HR, not pace or power**, in the first 10–14 days.
4. **Build in a recovery micro-cycle at days 10–14** of a 3-week camp.
5. **Take illness seriously.** A respiratory infection at altitude is not a minor inconvenience — it is a signal to stop and recover.
6. **Address sleep actively.** If periodic breathing or arousal is disrupting sleep, consult with a sports medicine physician before the camp escalates into NFOR.
